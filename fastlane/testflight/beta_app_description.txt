Echo turns your DRM-free audiobooks into a study tool: chapter looping, smart rewind, voice memo and photo bookmarks, a synced ePub/PDF reader with on-device auto-alignment, an Anki-style flashcard system (SM-2), and an Apple Watch remote you design yourself — up to 25 buttons across 5 pages, plus hands-free flashcard review and a Pomodoro timer on your wrist.

WHAT BETA MEANS

You're testing real, in-development software. Most things work well day-to-day (the developer uses Echo on a mail route every workday), but you will find rough edges — that's the point. Your books and data stay on your device, and it's wise to keep source files backed up as with any beta.

WHAT ECHO NEEDS FROM YOU

• Use it like you actually listen — commute, chores, workouts, bedtime. Real-life interruption is exactly what Echo is built for, so don't baby it.
• When something feels wrong, send feedback right then: take a screenshot, tap Share, and choose "Send Beta Feedback" — it arrives with your device and build info attached.
• For alignment issues, include the book title, narrator, and where the ePub/PDF came from (edition matters — narrators ad-lib and editions drift).
• Trackable bugs and feature ideas: github.com/dfakkeldy/Echo/issues

GOOD FIRST TESTS

1. Load a folder of MP3s or an M4B from Files/iCloud and listen through a few interruptions — does Smart Rewind put you back in context every time?
2. Add the matching ePub and run Auto-Align Chapters; then read along and tap a paragraph to jump the audio.
3. Make a bookmark with a voice memo and one with a photo — does your photo take over the player artwork when you pass it again?
4. Create a flashcard from a bookmark and do a Daily Review on your phone, then on your watch.
5. Design your watch button layout from the phone and drive a whole listening session from your wrist.

PRIVACY IN THE BETA

Echo itself contains no analytics, no tracking, and no servers — in beta exactly as in release. TestFlight (Apple's beta system) does share crash reports and the feedback you choose to send with the developer; that's TestFlight's standard behavior, not Echo's.
