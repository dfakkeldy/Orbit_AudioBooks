The Origin Story

Built on a mail route.

Echo's creator delivers mail — in and out of the car thirty times a shift, no CarPlay, an aux cable, and an AuDHD brain that learns from non-fiction audiobooks in stolen, interrupted minutes. No app on the internet would loop a single chapter until it stuck, stay ready to play on a watch after a long pause, or remember why a bookmark was made three driveways ago.

So he built it. 423 commits in eight weeks, nights and weekends around a full delivery route, with no formal programming background — an app for iPhone, Apple Watch, Mac, and CarPlay that treats audiobooks the way students treat textbooks. You can watch it being built, week by week.

Echo was designed for neurodivergent listeners first — and that's exactly why it works so well for everyone. Curb cuts were made for wheelchairs; they help every stroller, suitcase, and bicycle on the street. An app built for interrupted attention turns out to be what every commuter, student, and parent needed all along.

"I wanted to loop one chapter all day until I knew everything in it. No audiobook app would do it. A month later, I had built my own."
— Dan, Echo's creator · mail carrier · AuDHD

What Echo Does

A study system disguised as a player.

Every feature exists because of something we know about how memory works. The Learn page explains the science behind each one.

01

Smart Rewind

Hit play and Echo has already rewound — a little after a short pause, more after hours. Interruptions stop costing you context. The longer you're gone, the further it rewinds.

02

Chapter Looping

Repeat one chapter until you own it — the feature Echo was born for. Loop the playlist, the chapter, or the exact passage between two bookmarks.

03

Photo Bookmarks

Attach a photo of where you are; Echo switches the player art to your photo as you pass that moment again. Your brain encodes place alongside ideas — Echo turns that into a retrieval tool.

04

Voice Memo Bookmarks

Hold a button, speak your thought, keep driving. Your memos play back inline when the narration reaches them — past-you briefing present-you.

05

Built-in Spaced Repetition

An Anki-style flashcard system with audio on the cards, SM-2 scheduling, daily review, and hands-free review on Apple Watch. Never heard of Anki? We explain from zero.

06

Chapter Study Mode

Treat chapters like flashcards: listen, grade yourself, and Echo schedules each chapter's return. Your due chapters become today's listening queue.

07

Synced EPUB Reader

The text scrolls with the narration, diagrams appear when they're mentioned, and tapping a paragraph jumps the audio there. Search the whole book and leap to the spoken moment.

08

On-Device Auto-Alignment

Echo's speech recognition aligns text to audio in tiers — chapter snap, drift detection, word-level repair — entirely on your device. No cloud. Ever.

09

PDF Companions

Slides, scans, sheet music: align PDF pages to the audio with a precision scrubber joystick, and bookmark pages with exact zoom and position restore.

10

A Watch Remote You Design

Up to 25 buttons across 5 pages, arranged your way from the phone. Digital Crown scrubbing or volume. Big targets you can hit in the rain without looking. Plus a Pomodoro timer on your wrist.

11

Pristine Speed Control

Pitch-corrected speed from 0.5× up — narrators sound human at 1.25×. Every book remembers its own speed, and displayed times adjust to match.

12

Dyslexia-Friendly by Design

Lexend and OpenDyslexic fonts built in (this site is set in OpenDyslexic), Dynamic Type support, VoiceOver labels, and reduced-motion respect throughout.

Why It Works

Borrowed from a century of memory research.

Context-Dependent Memory

Your brain files where with what.

In a classic 1975 study, divers who learned underwater remembered best underwater. A photo of where you were is a key back into what you heard — that's photo bookmarks.

The Spacing Effect

Reviews timed to the forgetting curve.

Memory decays on a schedule; well-timed reviews flatten the curve. Echo's SM-2 scheduler shows you each card right before you'd forget it.

The Testing Effect

Recalling beats re-reading.

Struggling to retrieve an idea strengthens it far more than hearing it again. Grading yourself — Again, Hard, Good, Easy — is the workout.
